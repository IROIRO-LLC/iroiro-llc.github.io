How to Write Haiku: A Beginner's Guide to Japan's Shortest Poem

Three lines. Seventeen syllables. Centuries of tradition. Here is everything you need to start writing haiku today.

How to write haiku is one of those questions that sounds simple until you actually try it. Three lines, seventeen syllables, done. But anyone who has sat with a blank page trying to compress a feeling into that tiny frame knows there is more to it than counting on your fingers. Haiku is one of the oldest and most distilled forms of poetry in the world, and April, National Poetry Month's 30th anniversary, is the perfect time to learn it.

The form originated in Japan over four hundred years ago, and it has traveled remarkably well. Today haiku is written in dozens of languages by millions of people, from schoolchildren to Nobel laureates. Its appeal is its constraint. Where other forms of poetry give you room to wander, haiku demands you arrive somewhere in three breaths.

The 5-7-5 Syllable Structure

The most widely taught rule of haiku in English is the 5-7-5 syllable pattern: five syllables in the first line, seven in the second, five in the third. This gives you a total of seventeen syllables to work with, mirroring the seventeen on (sound units) of traditional Japanese haiku.

Here is a classic example from Matsuo Bashō, the poet most credited with elevating haiku into a serious literary art:

An old silent pond
A frog jumps into the pond —
Splash! Silence again.

The structure is not arbitrary. The first line sets a scene. The second line introduces an action or shift. The third line delivers a resolution or surprise. This arc, compressed into three lines, is what gives haiku its characteristic snap. You are not describing an event so much as catching a moment at the exact instant it changes.

A note on syllable counting: the Japanese on is not identical to an English syllable. Some modern haiku poets in English write shorter poems of ten to fourteen syllables to better approximate the brevity of the Japanese original. If you are just starting out, the 5-7-5 form is a solid foundation. You can loosen the rules once you have internalized the rhythm.

Kigo: The Seasonal Word

Traditional haiku almost always contains a kigo, a word or phrase that places the poem in a specific season. This is more than a setting detail. In Japanese poetic tradition, the season carries emotional weight. Spring suggests renewal and fragility. Summer carries energy and abundance. Autumn evokes impermanence. Winter implies stillness and solitude.

You do not need to memorize a formal kigo dictionary to write seasonal haiku. Pay attention to what is happening around you right now. In April, that might be cherry blossoms, birdsong at dawn, rain on new leaves, muddy shoes by the door. Each of these details anchors your poem in a moment that the reader can feel.

Some common kigo by season to get you started: spring brings cherry blossoms, swallows, plum rain, and melting snow; summer offers cicadas, sunflowers, heat haze, and fireworks; autumn carries harvest moon, falling leaves, persimmons, and migrating geese; winter holds first frost, bare branches, snow on bamboo, and warm sake.

The kigo does double duty. It tells the reader when they are, and it quietly suggests how to feel about what follows.

Kireji: The Cutting Word

The other essential element of traditional haiku is the kireji, or cutting word. In Japanese, specific particles like ya, kana, and keri create a grammatical pause or emotional break within the poem. English does not have exact equivalents, but the effect is often achieved with a dash, ellipsis, or simply a strong line break.

The cut divides the haiku into two parts that relate to each other in a way that is not immediately obvious. Think of it as a juxtaposition. One image is placed beside another, and the reader's mind naturally bridges the gap between them. That bridge is where the poem's meaning lives.

Consider this:

Spring rain falling —
the umbrella she left behind
still smells of her.

The dash after "falling" is the cut. Rain is one image. The umbrella and its scent are another. Together they create something neither image holds alone: the ache of missing someone, carried by a rainy day and a forgotten object. The poem does not say "I miss her." It does not need to.

Writing Your First Haiku

Enough theory. Here is a practical method for writing your first haiku, drawn from how experienced poets actually work.

Step one: go outside, or look out a window. Haiku begins with observation, not imagination. You are not inventing a scene. You are noticing one that already exists. What do you see, hear, or smell right now? A specific detail is always better than a general impression. Not "birds singing" but "a robin on the fence post." Not "nice weather" but "warm wind through the screen door."

Step two: find the moment of change. The best haiku capture something in motion, a transition between two states. A leaf detaching from a branch. The last light before dusk. Steam rising from a cup. Look for the instant where one thing becomes another.

Step three: write it long, then cut. Do not try to hit seventeen syllables on the first attempt. Write a sentence or two describing what you noticed. Then start removing words. Cut adjectives. Cut anything the reader can infer. Haiku rewards what is left unsaid as much as what is spoken.

Step four: count and adjust. Now shape what remains into three lines. Aim for the 5-7-5 pattern, but do not torture a good image to fit the count. If a natural, vivid poem lands at 5-7-4, most readers will not notice or care. The syllable structure is a guide, not a cage.

Step five: read it aloud. Haiku should feel like a single breath. Read your poem out loud and listen for the rhythm. Does it flow naturally? Does the pause between the second and third line create a small moment of surprise? If something sounds forced, revise until it doesn't.

Why Haiku Is Worth Journaling

There is a growing body of research on the therapeutic benefits of poetry writing. Studies show that writing poetry can reduce anxiety, lower physiological stress markers, and improve emotional processing. A systematic review published in Psychiatry Research found significant reductions in depressive symptoms, anxiety, and perceived stress across poetry-based interventions.

Haiku is uniquely suited to this because of its brevity. You do not need to carve out an hour for a writing session. A haiku can be written in two minutes on a lunch break, in a notes app on the train, or in a journal before bed. The constraint of three lines removes the pressure of producing something "significant." You are just capturing a moment.

Over time, a haiku journal becomes something remarkable: a record of what you actually noticed on any given day. Not what happened in the news or what your calendar held, but what the light looked like, what sound caught your attention, what small thing made you pause. It is a practice of attention that quietly reshapes how you move through ordinary days.

Japanese culture has a word for this kind of attentive awareness: mono no aware (物の哀れ), the bittersweet appreciation of impermanence. Every haiku is an exercise in mono no aware. You are writing down something that will never happen exactly this way again, and the act of noticing it is what gives it weight.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

A few patterns trip up nearly every beginning haiku writer. Knowing them in advance will save you some frustration.

Being too abstract. Haiku lives in the concrete. "Sadness fills my heart" is a statement. "Empty chair at dawn" is a haiku. Show the thing. Let the reader feel the emotion without being told what to feel.

Trying to be clever. Haiku is not a riddle or a punchline. The best haiku have a quality of simplicity that can feel almost too plain on first reading. The depth reveals itself on the second and third pass. Resist the urge to be surprising and aim instead to be true.

Cramming too much in. Seventeen syllables is not a lot of space. A haiku should contain one moment, not a narrative. If your poem has a beginning, middle, and end, it is probably a very short story, not a haiku. Pare it down to a single image or juxtaposition.

Ignoring the senses. The strongest haiku engage at least two senses. Sound and sight. Touch and smell. The interplay between senses creates dimensionality in a very small space. "Cold rain on tin roof" gives you temperature, sound, and setting in five syllables.

Starting Your Practice

The best way to learn haiku is to write one every day. It does not have to be good. It does not have to be shared. It just has to be honest. Step outside, notice something, write it down in three lines. Do that for a week, and you will start seeing the world differently. Do it for a month, during this National Poetry Month, and you will have thirty small records of a life lived with attention.

Bashō, who spent decades wandering Japan and writing haiku, once said: "Do not seek to follow in the footsteps of the wise. Seek what they sought." What the great haiku poets sought was not perfect syllable counts or clever wordplay. They sought presence. The ability to be fully where they were, when they were, and to hold that moment gently in a few short lines.

That is all haiku asks of you. Three lines. One moment. Your full attention.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the 5-7-5 rule in haiku?

The 5-7-5 rule refers to the traditional syllable structure of haiku in English: five syllables in the first line, seven in the second, and five in the third, totaling seventeen syllables. This structure mirrors the Japanese original, which uses seventeen on (sound units) across three phrases. While modern English-language haiku sometimes relaxes this count, the 5-7-5 form remains the most widely taught starting point for beginners.

Does a haiku have to be about nature?

Traditional Japanese haiku almost always references nature through a kigo (seasonal word). However, modern haiku in English has expanded to include urban life, emotions, and everyday moments. Many poets still use a nature element as an anchor, but it is not a strict requirement in contemporary practice.

What is a kigo in haiku?

A kigo is a seasonal reference word or phrase used in haiku to ground the poem in a specific time of year. Examples include "cherry blossoms" for spring, "cicadas" for summer, "harvest moon" for autumn, and "frost" for winter. Kigo give haiku emotional depth by connecting the reader to the rhythms of the natural world.

Can haiku be more than three lines?

In English, haiku is almost always written in three lines. The original Japanese form is traditionally written in a single vertical line with internal breaks. Some experimental poets write English haiku in one or two lines, but the three-line format is the standard and the best starting point for beginners.

What are the mental health benefits of writing haiku?

Research shows that poetry writing, including haiku, can reduce anxiety, lower stress markers, and improve emotional processing. Haiku's brevity makes it particularly accessible. The constraint of three lines encourages focused observation and present-moment awareness, which are core principles of mindfulness practice.
