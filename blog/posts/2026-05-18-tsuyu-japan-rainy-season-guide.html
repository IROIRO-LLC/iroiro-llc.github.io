Tsuyu: Japan's Rainy Season Explained (And Why It Might Be the Best Time to Visit)

Japan's "plum rain" season runs from early June to mid-July. Most travel guides treat it as a problem to schedule around. Here is the case for treating it as the destination.

Most Japan itineraries written in English skip June. Cherry blossoms are over. Autumn leaves are five months away. The forecast is a long row of cloud-and-raindrop icons. The advice, repeated across travel blogs and tour packages, is to wait.

This is one of the more reliable pieces of bad advice in modern travel writing.

Japan in June is not a worse version of Japan in October. It is a different Japan — quieter, cheaper, greener, with a specific kind of weather the country has organized its calendar, its poetry, and its temple gardens around for centuries. The Japanese word for that weather is tsuyu (梅雨, literally "plum rain"), and travelers who learn to plan around it rather than against it tend to come back with the most memorable trips.

Here is what tsuyu actually is, when it starts in 2026, what you can expect to see, and why a folding umbrella is the only piece of gear that matters.

What Tsuyu Actually Is

Tsuyu is the long band of overcast, frequently rainy weather that arrives between spring and summer across most of Japan. The character compound 梅雨 reads literally as "plum rain" because it lines up with the season ripe plums (ume) come down in mainland Japan. The "tsuyu" reading is the everyday spoken one; you will also see the season called baiu in formal or scientific contexts, which uses the same two characters with their on-yomi (Sino-Japanese) readings.

Three things distinguish tsuyu from a normal rainy stretch:

It is meteorologically defined. Tsuyu is created by a stationary weather front — the Baiu front — that forms where cool maritime polar air collides with warm, humid Pacific air. The front sits over Japan for roughly six weeks before drifting north into the Sea of Okhotsk and dissolving. This is why the Japan Meteorological Agency officially announces the start and end of tsuyu each year and tracks it like a season in its own right.

It is mostly drizzle, not deluge. The cliché image of rainy season is a tropical downpour. Real tsuyu is closer to a long Pacific Northwest spring — many days of light to moderate rain, often broken by hours of overcast calm or even brief sun. Total rainfall is high (often well above 200 mm for the month in June), but it falls slowly. Most days are walkable with an umbrella.

It skips Hokkaido. The Baiu front loses energy before reaching Japan's northernmost main island, and Hokkaido does not officially have a tsuyu season at all. If you want classic Japan-in-June scenery without the rain, the north of the country is the workaround locals use.

Tsuyu Dates for 2026, by Region

The JMA does not pre-announce exact start dates. It confirms onset within a few days of it actually happening, based on observed conditions. Long-range forecasts and 30-year averages give a good planning window, but the precise dates each year are decided by the weather itself.

Here is what we know so far for 2026:

Okinawa: Already in. The JMA announced tsuyu onset for Okinawa on May 4, 2026 — one day earlier than 2025 and six days earlier than the long-term average. Predicted end is around June 21. Okinawa's tsuyu is hotter and more tropical than the mainland's.

Kyushu and Tokai (Nagoya area): 30-year averages put tsuyu onset around June 4 and the end around July 19.

Honshu — Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka: Average onset around June 7, average end around July 19. These are the dates most international travelers will be planning around.

Tohoku (northern Honshu): Tsuyu enters last, typically in mid-June, and ends in late July.

Hokkaido: No official rainy season.

Long-range outlooks for 2026 suggest the season may run slightly wetter than average across mainland Japan, in line with the broader pattern of recent years. If you are deciding between an early-June and a late-June trip, late June will reliably be deeper into the rainy season, but it also lines up with the most spectacular hydrangea bloom.

What Actually Changes During Tsuyu

The headline practical effect: Japan empties out a little. Tsuyu sits in the gap between two of the country's two highest tourism peaks — cherry blossom season in late March through April, and the summer school-holiday window in late July through August. June occupies the dip in between.

Japan is in the middle of a tourism boom that does not look like it is slowing down. More than 20 million international visitors arrived in the first half of 2025; total inbound for 2025 cleared 39 million for the year, and 2026 is projected to land near 40 million. The bullet trains are crowded. The temples are crowded. The convenience-store onigiri shelves are crowded.

June is the cheapest, quietest month within that boom. Flight and hotel prices are noticeably lower than April or November. The lines at Fushimi Inari, Kiyomizu-dera, and the Arashiyama bamboo grove thin out. Major restaurants are easier to book on the day of. You will still see plenty of other tourists — Japan is not empty in June — but the density drops out of the bracket where every photo has someone else's selfie stick in it.

Beyond the crowd math, the rain itself changes what is worth seeing:

Hydrangeas (ajisai) hit peak bloom. Japan's hydrangea season runs from early June to mid-July, with mid- to late June as the bullseye. The flower's color saturates after rainfall, which is why every famous hydrangea temple is busiest on the day after a storm.

Moss gardens come alive. Kyoto's moss gardens — Saiho-ji (Koke-dera), Gio-ji, Sanzen-in — peak visually during tsuyu. The 120 varieties of moss at Saiho-ji require rainfall to reach their deepest greens. A dry June would be a worse June, not a better one, for this kind of scenery.

Cities soften. Kyoto in particular reads differently under cloud and rain. Stone temple paths darken. Lanterns glow earlier in the day. The visual density of a Japanese garden — designed across centuries to be read in mist as well as sun — is closer to its intended state when the air is wet.

Indoor culture becomes the main event. Tea ceremonies, sake breweries, soba and ramen shops, the small back-street izakaya you ducked into to get out of the rain, kissaten cafés, Studio Ghibli Park, teamLab, the National Museums in Tokyo and Kyoto — Japan has a full indoor itinerary for anyone willing to shift their plan inside for an afternoon. A rainy day routes you toward the parts of the country travelers often run out of time to see.

Tsuyu in Japanese Poetry and Daily Life

Tsuyu is not just a weather pattern. It is a cultural season that Japanese literature has been writing about for more than a thousand years.

In haiku, "tsuyu" is one of the most-used seasonal words (kigo) for early summer. The Japanese poetic tradition treats the rainy season as a particular kind of beauty: melancholic, introspective, soft, oddly intimate. Rain in haiku does not stand for sadness exactly. It stands for the quiet awareness of time passing — the same family of feeling as the famous Japanese concept of mono no aware, the gentle pathos of impermanence.

The standard imagery is small and specific. A frog moving across wet stone. The smell of tatami in a damp house. A hydrangea outside a window. Bashō's "old pond, frog jumps in" haiku is often misread as a still summer scene; it is more accurately a rainy-season image, with everything that implies about quiet, water, and stillness.

Daily life in Japan during tsuyu has its own quiet rituals. The umbrella appears at the door of every shop and restaurant. Plastic umbrella sleeves are stationed at entrances to keep floors dry. Conbini convenience stores stock disposable rain ponchos at the front. Convenience-store coffee improves perceptibly. People in offices begin saying "atsui desu ne" (it's hot) as a greeting even when it is not particularly hot, because the humidity makes everything feel five degrees warmer than it is.

The cultural posture toward the rain is not resignation. It is something closer to attentiveness. The rain is the season. You wear the right shoes, carry the right umbrella, take a long look at the hydrangeas, and let your trip slow down.

Where to Go During Tsuyu

If you are building a June itinerary, these are the places the rain actively makes better.

Kamakura — Meigetsu-in and Hasedera

An hour south of Tokyo on the Yokosuka Line. Meigetsu-in is known as the "Hydrangea Temple," with over 2,500 blue-tinted ajisai lining the stone steps to the main hall. Hasedera, the other big hydrangea draw in town, sets thousands of plants along a hillside path that climbs the back of the temple grounds. Go on a weekday morning. Bring waterproof shoes — the stone steps get slick.

Kyoto — Mimuroto-ji in Uji

About 30 minutes from central Kyoto. Around 20,000 hydrangea plants in dozens of varieties spread across a hillside garden — large enough to absorb crowds, vivid enough to be worth the trip even on a downpour day. Kyoto-area locals call Mimuroto-ji Ajisai-dera, "the hydrangea temple." The temple lights the garden in the evening on select dates during peak bloom.

Hakone — The Tozan Railway

Hakone's mountain railway, the Hakone Tozan Line, climbs through hillsides planted with hydrangeas the entire route between Hakone-Yumoto and Gora. Mid- to late June peak. The railway runs special illuminated night trains during the bloom, which are some of the more photographable train rides in Japan. Pair with an onsen day for the platonic ideal of a tsuyu afternoon.

Kyoto — The Moss Gardens

Saiho-ji (Koke-dera) requires an advance reservation by postcard and includes a short sutra-copying session before you enter the moss garden. Gio-ji is smaller, easier to access, and almost as good. Sanzen-in in Ohara, a little farther out, has both moss and hydrangeas. Any of these is at its visual peak during tsuyu specifically because of the rain.

Tokyo — Hakusan Shrine and Asukayama

Tokyo's underrated hydrangea spots. Hakusan Shrine hosts the Bunkyo Hydrangea Festival in mid-June with about 3,000 plants and food stalls. The Asuka-no-Komichi path along the base of Asukayama Park in Kita ward runs roughly 400 meters of hydrangeas and is much less photographed than Kamakura.

Hokkaido — The Tsuyu-Free Option

If the idea of two weeks of rain genuinely will not work for your trip, fly into Sapporo. Hokkaido is the one major part of the country the Baiu front does not reach. June in Hokkaido is mid-spring temperatures, long daylight hours, lavender beginning to flower in Furano, and almost no rain. The trade-off is that you are skipping the cultural heart of mainland Japan.

What to Pack

Tsuyu travel rewards a small, deliberate kit.

A compact folding umbrella. Japanese convenience stores sell clear plastic ones for under 1,000 yen if you forget — they are everywhere, and there is no shame in using one. A folding travel umbrella from home is lighter and more durable for a full trip.

Waterproof walking shoes. Not sandals (you will be walking through puddles), not running shoes (they soak through and stay soaked in 80% humidity), not new leather (it ruins). Waterproof low-cut hiking shoes or sneakers with treated uppers are the right tool. Whatever you bring, break them in before you fly.

A lightweight rain jacket. Mostly as backup. An umbrella handles 90% of tsuyu rain better than a jacket — it keeps your face dry, you can put it down indoors, and it does not make you sweat. The jacket is for the rare day the wind is bad enough that the umbrella becomes a sail.

Quick-dry layers. Cotton stays wet. Synthetics and merino wool do not. Bring fewer pieces of better fabric and plan to wear things twice. Japan's hotel laundry options are good; coin laundries are everywhere.

A small towel. Japanese restrooms often do not have paper towels — people carry a small cloth handkerchief (hankachi) for drying their hands. A small towel is also useful for blotting rain off your face, your phone, and your hair before stepping into a restaurant. You can buy charming ones in any department store the day you land.

A dry pouch for your phone. A simple zip-top freezer bag works. Your phone is your map, translator, train timetable, and camera; it does not love sustained humidity and unexpected rain.

Offline tools. Most travelers underestimate how often Japanese rural Wi-Fi, mountain shrines, and subway transfers will leave them temporarily without a signal. Download Google Maps offline tiles for the cities and regions you will visit, save an offline translation pack in your translation app of choice, and screenshot the kanji for your hotel, your station, and any allergens you cannot eat. The 2025 wave of on-device AI on phones has made offline translation, transcription, and map-guidance dramatically better than even two years ago — if your phone supports it, lean on it. The Contrarian Case for June

Here is the argument compressed.

The Japan you have seen on Instagram is mostly two seasons of it — pink in April, red and gold in November. Those months are also the most expensive, the most crowded, and the most photographed. Every shot you take has been taken ten million times. The temples are the same temples. The streets are the same streets. You will see them, beautifully, through a layer of other people doing the same thing.

Tsuyu is the country a little more to itself. The hydrangeas bloom whether anyone shows up or not. The moss gardens fill in. Kyoto's rainy lanes look the way the Edo-period prints painted them. You get more conversations with shop owners because their shops are not overrun. You eat better dinners because reservations relax. You spend less. You walk slower. You carry an umbrella.

And if you let it, the rain teaches you something the dry months are too busy to teach. The Japanese aesthetic tradition has been arguing for centuries that mist, weathering, asymmetry, and the visible passage of time are not problems to fix. They are what beauty actually looks like once you stop sanding it. June is the month of the year when the country agrees with itself about that.

Bring an umbrella. Plan one indoor afternoon for every two outdoor ones. Pack a small towel. Book Mimuroto-ji for the third week of the month. Then let Japan be the version of itself it is in the rain.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is tsuyu in Japan?

Tsuyu (梅雨, literally "plum rain") is Japan's rainy season — the period of frequent, often steady rain that sits between spring and summer across most of the country. It typically runs from early June to mid-July in Honshu, Shikoku, and Kyushu, starts earlier in Okinawa, and skips Hokkaido entirely. Total monthly rainfall during tsuyu commonly exceeds 200 mm, but the rain usually arrives as long stretches of light to moderate drizzle rather than tropical-style downpours.

When does tsuyu start in 2026?

Okinawa entered tsuyu earlier than average in 2026 — the Japan Meteorological Agency announced onset on May 4, 2026, with a predicted end around June 21. For the rest of the country, the JMA goes by 30-year averages: Kyushu and the Tokai region typically begin around June 4, the main Honshu cities (Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka) around June 7, and northern Honshu in mid-June. The rainy season generally ends in mid- to late July. The JMA does not pre-announce exact start dates; it confirms onset within a few days of it actually happening.

Is it a bad time to visit Japan during tsuyu?

Not really. Tsuyu rain is usually light and intermittent rather than continuous. In exchange you get noticeably smaller crowds at major temples and shrines, lower flight and hotel prices than peak summer or autumn, the hydrangea (ajisai) bloom at its peak, moss gardens at their most vivid, and a softer, quieter version of cities like Kyoto. Travelers who plan for rain rather than against it often consider June one of the most rewarding months in Japan.

What should I pack for Japan in June?

A compact folding umbrella, waterproof walking shoes (not sandals), a lightweight rain jacket, quick-drying clothing layers, a small dry bag or zip pouch for your phone, and a handkerchief or small towel — Japanese restrooms often do not have paper towels, and a small towel is also useful for drying your hands and face on humid days. Temperatures in June run roughly 22–28°C (72–82°F) with high humidity, so breathable fabrics matter more than warm ones.

Where are the best places to see hydrangeas during tsuyu?

Kamakura (Meigetsu-in, with over 2,500 blue-tinted hydrangeas along its stone steps, and Hasedera, where thousands line the hillside path), Kyoto's Mimuroto-ji in Uji (around 20,000 plants, known as "Ajisai-dera" — the hydrangea temple), Hakone (the Hakone Tozan Railway runs hydrangea-lined tracks and special illuminated night trains in peak season), Tokyo's Hakusan Shrine (about 3,000 plants and the Bunkyo Hydrangea Festival), and Gamagori's Ajisai-no-Sato near Nagoya (around 50,000 plants, one of the largest displays in the country). Peak bloom is usually mid- to late June.

Does it rain every day during tsuyu?

No. Many tsuyu days are overcast without rain, and even rainy days are typically broken by hours of dry overcast or brief sunshine. Heavy all-day rain happens but is not the norm. The pattern is closer to "rain at some point most days" than "rain all day every day." Planning one indoor activity per day as a default usually means you will use it three or four times across a two-week trip.
