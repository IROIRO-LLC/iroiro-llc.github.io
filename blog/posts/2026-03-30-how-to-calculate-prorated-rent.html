How to Calculate Prorated Rent

Three simple methods for figuring out exactly what you owe when moving in or out mid-month — with formulas, real examples, and common mistakes to avoid.

How to calculate prorated rent is one of those questions that sounds simple until you actually need the answer. You signed a lease starting April 15th. Your landlord says you owe a partial month. But how much, exactly? And whose math do you trust — yours, your landlord's, or the internet's?

Prorated rent is the fair portion of a month's rent based on the number of days you actually occupy a unit. Whether you are moving into your first apartment, relocating for a new job, or ending a lease early, understanding how proration works protects you from overpaying and helps landlords keep their books clean.

The good news: the math is straightforward once you know which method to use. The tricky part is that there are multiple accepted methods, and the one that applies to you may depend on where you live.

The Basic Prorated Rent Formula

Every prorated rent calculation follows the same core logic. You find a daily rent rate, then multiply it by the number of days you will occupy the unit. The formula looks like this:

Prorated Rent = (Monthly Rent ÷ Days in Period) × Days Occupied

The variable that changes between methods is "Days in Period." That single number is where most of the confusion lives, and where the three common calculation methods diverge.

Method 1: Actual Days in the Month

This is the most intuitive approach. You divide the monthly rent by the actual number of days in the specific month you are moving in or out. January has 31 days, April has 30, February has 28 or 29.

Say your monthly rent is $1,800 and you move in on April 15th. April has 30 days, and you will live there from the 15th through the 30th — that is 16 days.

$1,800 ÷ 30 = $60.00 per day
$60.00 × 16 = $960.00 prorated rent

Now imagine the same move-in date but in January, a 31-day month. The numbers shift slightly:

$1,800 ÷ 31 = $58.06 per day
$58.06 × 17 = $987.02 prorated rent

The actual-days method is popular because it feels fair. You are paying for exactly the slice of the month you use, calculated against the real length of that month. Most landlords and property management companies default to this approach.

Method 2: The Banker's Month (30-Day Standard)

The banker's method simplifies things by treating every month as if it has exactly 30 days, regardless of the calendar. This creates a consistent daily rate that never changes from month to month.

Using the same $1,800 rent with an April 15th move-in:

$1,800 ÷ 30 = $60.00 per day
$60.00 × 16 = $960.00 prorated rent

For April, the result is identical to Method 1 because April actually has 30 days. But in a 31-day month like March, the banker's method produces a slightly different number than the actual-days method because you are still dividing by 30.

This method matters for legal reasons. In California, for example, the banker's 30-day method is the standard used for prorated rent calculations. If you are renting in a state with specific proration rules, the banker's method may not be optional — it may be required.

Method 3: Annual Daily Rate (365-Day Method)

The most precise method calculates your daily rent based on the entire year rather than a single month. You multiply the monthly rent by 12 to get the annual rent, then divide by 365 (or 366 in a leap year).

$1,800 × 12 = $21,600 per year
$21,600 ÷ 365 = $59.18 per day
$59.18 × 16 = $946.85 prorated rent

This approach produces the most mathematically consistent daily rate because it smooths out the variation between 28-day, 30-day, and 31-day months. Some corporate landlords and commercial lease agreements use this method for that reason.

In practice, though, the 365-day method is less common for residential leases. The difference is usually small — in our example, it saves the tenant about $13 compared to the actual-days method — and most people find the monthly calculation more intuitive.

Which Method Should You Use?

The short answer: check your lease first, then check your local laws.

Your lease agreement should specify how prorated rent is calculated. If it does, that is the method that applies to your situation. If the lease is silent on the question, local and state regulations may dictate a default. In the absence of both, the actual-days method is the most widely accepted standard.

As a tenant, it is worth doing the math yourself regardless of which method your landlord uses. The calculation takes less than a minute, and catching a discrepancy before you sign a check is far easier than disputing it after the fact.

Move-In vs. Move-Out Proration

Prorated rent works the same way whether you are arriving or leaving. The only difference is how you count the days.

For a move-in, count from your move-in date through the last day of the month. If you move in on April 15th in a 30-day month, that is 16 days (April 15 through April 30, inclusive).

For a move-out, count from the first of the month through your last day in the unit. If your lease ends on April 20th, that is 20 days (April 1 through April 20, inclusive).

One common point of confusion: your move-in or move-out date counts as a day you occupy the unit. If you pick up keys on April 15th, April 15th is day one of your occupancy, even if you do not sleep there that night. Similarly, if you return keys on April 20th, that day typically counts as your last day of occupancy.

Common Mistakes to Watch For

The math itself is simple, but a few common errors can lead to overpaying or underpaying.

Forgetting to count the move-in day. If you move in on the 15th and the month has 30 days, you have 16 days of occupancy, not 15. Count the 15th as day one.

Using the wrong number of days in the month. It sounds obvious, but double-check. September has 30 days, not 31. February in a leap year has 29. A one-day error on a $2,000 rent can mean $65 or more.

Mixing up methods mid-calculation. If your landlord uses the banker's 30-day method, do not divide by the actual days in the month. Pick one method and apply it consistently.

Not accounting for additional fees. Prorated rent covers base rent, but what about parking, pet fees, or storage? Some landlords prorate these additional charges as well, while others charge them in full for the partial month. Your lease should clarify this, but if it does not, ask before you sign.

When to Negotiate Prorated Rent

Not every landlord prorates rent automatically, and in many jurisdictions they are not legally required to. But that does not mean you cannot ask.

The strongest negotiating position is during the lease-signing process, before you have committed. If a landlord wants you to move in on the 15th but expects a full month's rent, point out that you are willing to pay a fair rate for the days you occupy the unit. Most reasonable landlords will agree, especially in a competitive rental market where vacancies cost them more than a few days of prorated rent.

Move-out proration is trickier. If your lease ends mid-month, proration is usually built into the agreement. But if you are breaking a lease early or negotiating an early termination, prorated rent for your final month can be part of that conversation. Having the math ready — knowing exactly what a fair partial month costs — gives you credibility in that discussion.

Why This Matters More in Peak Moving Season

Spring and summer are the busiest times of year for apartment moves. According to Apartment List research, rental demand climbs sharply from April through August, driven by job relocations, graduations, and families timing moves around the school year. That means millions of new leases start on dates other than the first of the month.

In a tight rental market, landlords have less incentive to negotiate. But understanding proration protects you regardless. Even if the landlord sets the terms, knowing whether their calculation is accurate means you are paying exactly what you owe — not a dollar more.

For landlords and property managers, getting proration right is equally important. An incorrect proration calculation can lead to tenant disputes, accounting headaches, and in some states, legal liability. The few minutes it takes to verify the math upfront can save hours of back-and-forth later.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is prorated rent?

Prorated rent is a partial month's rent calculated based on the exact number of days you occupy a rental unit. Instead of paying for a full month when you move in or out mid-month, you pay only for the days you actually live there. The calculation divides the monthly rent into a daily rate, then multiplies by your days of occupancy.

How do you calculate prorated rent for a mid-month move-in?

Divide your monthly rent by the number of days in the month to get a daily rate, then multiply by the number of days you will occupy the unit. For example, if rent is $1,800 and you move in on April 15th: $1,800 ÷ 30 days = $60 per day × 16 days = $960. Remember to count your move-in day as the first day of occupancy.

Are landlords required to prorate rent?

It depends on your location. Some states and municipalities require landlords to prorate rent when a tenant occupies a unit for less than a full month, while others leave it to the terms of the lease agreement. Always check your local tenant rights laws and read your lease carefully before assuming proration applies.

What is the difference between the actual days method and the banker's method?

The actual days method divides rent by the real number of days in the specific month — 28, 29, 30, or 31. The banker's method always divides by 30, regardless of the actual month length. Both are widely accepted, but the banker's method is legally required in some states like California. The difference is usually small but can matter for shorter months like February.

Does prorated rent apply to move-out as well as move-in?

Yes. Prorated rent applies any time you occupy a rental for less than a full month, whether you are arriving or departing. If your lease ends on the 20th, you should only owe rent for those 20 days. The same formula applies: daily rate multiplied by the number of days you occupied the unit.
