Best Apps for Writing Haiku in 2026: A Buyer's Guide

There is no single best haiku app. There is a best one for what you actually want to do. Here is an honest comparison, organized by use case, of the strongest options for iPhone and Android this year.

The short answer: the best haiku app depends on your goal. For keeping a daily writing habit and sharing finished poems beautifully, 17on is the strongest free pick on iOS and Android. For reading and studying the classics, the nonprofit THF Haiku app is unmatched. For a bare-bones syllable counter with no account, Haiku: Poems Made Simple works offline. For social motivation, try Haikutopia. If you want AI to write the poem for you, an AI poem generator will do it, though that is the opposite of practicing the form.

Haiku looks simple, which is exactly why an app helps. Three lines, seventeen syllables in the traditional form, and a surprising amount of craft hiding inside that small frame. The hard parts are not obvious to a beginner: counting English syllables accurately, building a daily habit so the practice actually sticks, and learning the form by reading good examples. Different apps solve different parts of that problem, and most people pick the wrong one because they searched "best haiku app" and grabbed whatever was at the top.

So this guide is organized the way you would actually decide: by what you are trying to do. We build one of these apps ourselves (17on), and we will be upfront about where it is the right choice and where something else fits you better. An honest comparison is more useful to you, and frankly it is the only kind worth writing.

How to Choose: Five Questions

Before the list, the five questions that decide everything:

1. Do you want to write, or to read? Some apps are archives of famous haiku for study. Some are blank editors for your own work. A few do both. These are genuinely different products.

2. One-off poems, or an ongoing journal? A notepad clears. A journal accumulates. If you want a haiku practice rather than the occasional poem, a journal-first app changes the experience completely.

3. Solo, or social? Some apps are quiet and private. Others are built around prompts, voting, leaderboards, and a community. Neither is better. They suit different temperaments.

4. Do you want help with syllables? English syllable counting trips up almost everyone ("fire" is one syllable or two depending on who you ask). A real, dictionary-based counter is the most useful single feature for a beginner.

5. Do you want to write it yourself, or have AI do it? This is the biggest fork. If the goal is to improve, AI generation defeats the purpose. If you just want a quick novelty, it is fine.

The Best Haiku Apps, by Use Case

Best for a daily writing habit and sharing: 17on

17on (pronounced "seventeen-on") is a haiku poetry journal, which is the important word. It is built less as a one-time composer and more as a place to keep a continuous practice. Every poem is saved, dated, and searchable, so over weeks the app becomes a record of small noticed moments rather than a notepad you keep emptying.

The writing experience is the strongest part. The syllable counter is dictionary-based, drawing on more than 126,000 English words, and it gives real-time, line-by-line feedback as you write rather than a rough estimate. It supports five short forms, not just one: Traditional (5-7-5), Modern (4-4-6), Lune (5-3-5), Monoku (a single line), and Freeform. That range lets the form grow with you instead of boxing you in.

When a poem is done, you can share it on designed stationery, with different papers, pen styles, and ink colors that you collect as you write and explore. There is an inspiration feed of famous haiku to learn from, and an optional on-device AI assist where the hardware supports it, which nudges rather than writes the whole poem for you. The journal and the counting stay on your device.

Cost: free to start, including 31 haiku, the full journal and search, sharing, the stationery collection, and the inspiration feed, with a paid upgrade for unlimited writing and the full set of styles. Platforms: iOS and Android. Best for: anyone who wants the habit to stick, wants their finished poems to look as considered as they feel, and likes reading famous haiku for inspiration alongside their own work. Not ideal if: you want a purely social, competitive experience built around leaderboards and voting.

Best for reading and studying the classics: THF Haiku

THF Haiku, from The Haiku Foundation, is the reference app. It puts a large, searchable archive of haiku from poets around the world in your pocket, searchable by author, source, or content. It is a study and reading tool first, made by a nonprofit dedicated to the form, and it is free.

What it is not is a journaling or composition app, and it does not try to be. Its archive is deeper than the inspiration feed inside a writing app, so many writers keep THF Haiku for wide reading and a separate app like 17on for daily writing: read broadly in one, practice in the other. Best for: students of the form and anyone who learns by immersion in great examples. Not ideal if: your main goal is to write and keep your own work.

Best no-frills syllable counter: Haiku: Poems Made Simple

If you want the lightest possible tool, Haiku: Poems Made Simple is a clean editor with a syllable counter, a built-in library of classic poems for inspiration, and the considerable virtues of working offline and requiring no signup. It does one job and gets out of the way. Best for: minimalists who want to count syllables and write, with nothing else on screen. Not ideal if: you want an accumulating journal, multiple forms, or polished sharing.

Best for social motivation: Haikutopia

Some people write more when other people are watching. Haikutopia turns haiku into a daily, gentle competition: a shared prompt, real-time syllable counting, community voting on submissions, a global leaderboard, and streak tracking to keep you coming back. It is the most extroverted option on this list. Best for: writers who are motivated by prompts, streaks, and a community. Not ideal if: you want a quiet, private practice or you write on your own schedule.

Best if you want AI to write it: Haiku AI generators

Apps such as Haiku: AI Poem Generator will take a topic and return a finished 5-7-5 poem in seconds. For a quick novelty, a greeting-card line, or a starting point you then edit, that is genuinely handy. But be clear-eyed about the trade: the app is doing the writing, which is the one thing you cannot outsource if your goal is to get better. Best for: one-off novelty poems and quick prompts. Not ideal if: you want to develop your own craft. For that, a journaling app that assists rather than generates serves you far better.

Best for collaborative writing: JamVerse

JamVerse is built around writing poetry together, with poets connecting to build verse collaboratively. It is a niche but real use case, most appealing to groups, classrooms, and writing circles. Best for: collaborative and group writing. Not ideal if: you want a solo journal.

Honorable mentions: Maiku is a tidy iOS composer with a syllable counter and text or image sharing, and general poetry apps like Mirakee handle haiku alongside quotes and longer poems if you want one app for everything.

A Note on Why the Form Is Worth the Effort

It is easy to treat haiku as a syllable puzzle. The reason it has survived four centuries is that it is a training device for attention. The form forces you to notice one ordinary thing clearly enough to fit it into seventeen syllables: a wet leaf, a held breath, the sound of a train you cannot see. That is the same slow, deliberate noticing behind so much of what we find worth keeping, from mono no aware, the quiet appreciation of impermanence, to shinrin-yoku, the practice of slow sensory time among trees.

An app cannot give you that attention. What a good one can do is lower the friction enough that you actually write, every day, until the noticing becomes a habit. If you are brand new to the form, start with our guide on how to write haiku, then pick the app from this list that matches what you are trying to do.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best free haiku app?

It depends on what you want to do. For keeping a daily writing habit and sharing finished poems, 17on is the strongest free pick on iOS and Android. It is free to start with 31 haiku, a syllable counter, five short-form styles, and collectible sharing stationery. For reading and studying classic haiku, the nonprofit THF Haiku app is free and unmatched as an archive. For a bare-bones syllable counter with no account, Haiku: Poems Made Simple works offline at no cost.

Is there a haiku app with a syllable counter?

Yes. 17on counts syllables in real time, line by line, using a dictionary of more than 126,000 English words rather than a rough guess, and it supports the 5-7-5 traditional form plus four other short-form styles. Haiku: Poems Made Simple and Maiku also include syllable counters in a simpler editor. A live counter is the single most useful feature for beginners, because English syllable counts are not always obvious.

What is the best haiku app for keeping a journal?

17on is built specifically as a haiku journal rather than a one-off composer. Every poem you write is saved, searchable, and dated, so the app becomes an ongoing record of small moments rather than a notepad you clear. It pairs the journal with a syllable counter, an inspiration feed of famous haiku, and shareable styled cards. If your goal is a sustained daily practice, a journal-first app matters more than any single feature.

Is there an app just for reading famous haiku?

Yes. THF Haiku, made by The Haiku Foundation, is a free app built around a large searchable archive of haiku from poets around the world, searchable by author, source, or content. It is a reading and study tool first, not a writing or journaling app. If you also want to write and keep your own work, a journaling app like 17on includes its own inspiration feed of famous haiku alongside the editor, so many writers use the two together.

Are there AI apps that write haiku for you?

Yes. Apps like Haiku: AI Poem Generator will turn a prompt into a finished 5-7-5 poem. They are useful for a quick novelty or a starting point, but they do the writing for you, which is the opposite of practicing the form. If your aim is to get better at writing haiku yourself, choose a journaling app with guidance, such as 17on, which also offers an optional on-device AI assist where supported rather than generating the whole poem.

Do haiku apps work offline?

Many do. Haiku: Poems Made Simple works fully offline with no signup. 17on keeps your journal and syllable counting on your device and offers on-device AI haiku assistance where the hardware supports it, so the core writing experience does not depend on a connection. Social apps such as Haikutopia and JamVerse need a connection for their community features, since voting, prompts, and collaboration happen online.

What is the best haiku app for iPhone and Android?

17on is available on both iOS and Android, which makes it the most portable choice if you move between platforms or want the same journal on a phone and a tablet. Several popular options, including Maiku and many App Store composers, are iOS only. THF Haiku is also broadly available for reading. Check your store before committing, since availability changes.
